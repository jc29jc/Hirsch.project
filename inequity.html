01 // The Inequity

Welcome to the Inequity page. Before we jump into numbers or statistics, we need to understand the human side of this problem. When someone leaves prison, everyday tasks that seem simple to us like finding a good place to sleep or getting a job turn into huge challenges for these ex-convicts. This page will break down the major barriers that returning citizens face the second they get out of prison.

  • 01

    What hard struggles do people face the exact moment they get out of prison?

    The Job and Housing Trap

    It is almost impossible to build a normal life because landlords and bosses use instant background checks to throw away applications from anyone with a bad record. This creates a ripple effect. You cannot get a job without a home, but you cannot pay for a home without a job, leaving you hopeless.

    The Starting Money Gap

    Most people leave prison with almost no money, sometimes just $50 and a bus ticket. Since they have no cash and no safety net, they struggle to buy basic things like food and clothes. This extreme poverty forces many people to commit minor survival crimes just to stay alive, leading to quick rearrests.

    Strict Government Rules

    The law actively blocks people with past mistakes from getting help. For example, strict federal rules can permanently ban people with certain records from living in affordable, government-subsidized public housing, even though it is exactly what they need to avoid homelessness.

    Mental Toll and a Changed World

    Being locked up causes deep mental trauma, like severe anxiety, depression, and a lack of trust in society. On top of that, people who have been away for a long time return to a completely different world. Without any help, they have to figure out how to use modern things they’ve never seen before, like smartphones or electronic subway cards.
