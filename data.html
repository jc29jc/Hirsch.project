  • 01

    What is the unemployment rate for people with a record compared to everyone else?

    The Job Rate

    According to the Prison Policy Initiative, ex convicts face an unemployment rate of over 27%. In comparison, the general publics rate of unemployment is 4%, which is 5x less then the unemployment rate of ex-convicts. To make it even worse, this level of unemployment is worse than the worst years of the Great Depression. And people might think that this is probably because most ex-convicts don't want to work, but this is not true. Over 93% of released prisoners are looking for jobs compared to 84% of the general public. This bad rate for these prisoners are because ex-convicts face a 50% rate of not getting a job. When they do get a job, they are still discriminated, and earn about 40% less money then their peers.

  • 02

    How many people end up getting arrested again after 3-5 years of release?

    Re-arrests for ex-convicts

    According to USA Facts.org, numbers show that without proper help from the community to get ex-convicts back into life, a lot of incarcerated people get re-arrested back into jail within 3-5 years. (This info is based on police contact and trouble with the police, not thrown right back into jail.)

    1. Re-arrests in three years: About 68% of released people get re-arrested within three years of leaving jail

    2. Re-arrests in five years: in a five year period, the percentage of re-arrests climbs to 76%

    3. Custody Return: While this information is just based on police contact, the actual rate of released prisoners doing another crime to get them thrown behind bars is also super high, with a 39-50% of ex-convicts going back to jail.

    Key Re-Arrest Factors

    The three main factors of re-arrest rely on these three things:

    1. Age: Younger ex-convicts face the highest re-arrest rates, while older people (specifically ages 65 and up) have a way lower risk of returning to prison.

    2. Type of crime that locked you up in the first place: People that were locked up for property crimes or theft have a higher re-arrest rate than any other crime.

    3. Location: The state or community changes the re-arrest numbers. Poorer communities have larger re-arrests than better communities with good job opportunities or support programs.
